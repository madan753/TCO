How are the numbers calculated?
  • Running cost — distance × rate. EV: (kWh/km × electricity rate). Petrol: (litres/km × fuel price).
  • Battery degradation — EV range drops ~5% per 10,000 km, so electricity use slowly rises.
  • Engine wear — petrol mileage drops ~5% per 15,000 km, so fuel use slowly rises.
  • Service cost — drifts up ~2% per 10,000 km as parts age.
  • Net cost = price + running + service + insurance + bluebook − resale (if included).
  • Per-km cost = net cost ÷ total distance, so two bikes with different usage can be compared fairly.

Quick answers

Why are EV and petrol usage separate?

Because real riders don't use them the same way. EV is so cheap per km that owners ride more; petrol owners often cut back when fuel is expensive. Forcing the same km for both would hide this real-world difference.

Is EV always cheaper in Nepal?

No. If you ride very little (under ~3,000 km/year), the EV's higher purchase price may never pay back. This tool shows the breakeven point so you know exactly when — or if — the EV pulls ahead.

What's a fair comparison when ownership years differ?

Look at the per-km cost in the KPI grid — it normalises for different distances. The chart holds each bike's cost flat after its ownership period so you can still see the lines side by side.

How accurate are Nepal prices?

Defaults are based on 2024 Nepali dealer prices: petrol ~रू175/L, electricity ~रू9.8/kWh (NEA domestic tier), bluebook ~रू2,500/yr, insurance ~रू2,800/yr. All are editable in step 5 to match your quote.

Does this include home charging setup?

Not by default. Add it to the EV's on-road price if your dealer charges separately. Most EV bikes in Nepal come with a portable charger included.

What about battery replacement?

Not included as a separate line item — most EV bike batteries last 5–7 years and the resale value already reflects battery wear. If you expect to replace the battery mid-ownership, add that to the EV's annual service cost.
